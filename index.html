Seizing Iran's Uranium: The Physics of an Impossible Operation

How do you extract 400kg of weapons-grade nuclear material from sealed tunnels deep inside a hostile nation — when the material itself could kill you?

Developing Pentagon deploying Marine Expeditionary Unit to Middle East ▸

A Marine Expeditionary Unit (~2,500 Marines and sailors) is being sent to the Middle East, per CNN and the Wall Street Journal. MEUs are rapid-response forces designed for large-scale evacuations, amphibious operations, ship-to-shore raids and assaults. They include ground and aviation combat components, and some units are trained for special operations.

While the Trump administration says there are "no current plans" for ground troops in Iran, they have refused to rule it out. The MEU gives commanders additional options for a range of contingencies.

An MEU is not a nuclear seizure force — but it is the kind of unit that secures perimeters, holds airfields, and provides the logistics backbone for exactly the kind of operation described below.

The Chemistry That Makes It Lethal

UF₆
Uranium Hexafluoride
The form in which enriched uranium is stored. Gaseous when heated, solid at room temperature. Highly reactive with moisture.
HF
Hydrogen Fluoride
Released when UF₆ is dispersed. Penetrates skin on contact, destroys tissue, attacks bone calcium. Lethal in small concentrations in enclosed spaces.
UO₂F₂
Uranyl Fluoride
Forms when moisture contacts UF₆ during transport. Toxic and radioactive solid. The reaction is exothermic — generates heat that can cause container rupture.

The Hidden Danger: Criticality

The 19 cylinders of UF₆ are kept physically separated by piping infrastructure. This isn't just plumbing — the geometry of the arrangement is the safety system. If enough fissile material is brought together in the wrong configuration, it reaches criticality: an uncontrolled, self-sustaining nuclear chain reaction.

This isn't a nuclear detonation — but it produces a lethal burst of neutron and gamma radiation that would kill anyone nearby within hours or days. Special forces teams that don't understand this and begin dismantling piping to access cylinders could trigger exactly this event.

"The special ops team MUST understand that the piping structures must not be removed."
— Cheryl Rofer, former nuclear researcher, Los Alamos National Laboratory

The Bottom Line

"There's no doubt that the US can do it. They're probably the only military in the world that could. But you either do it incredibly small and insert in a very covert way, or you go in at scale — you essentially turn that part of Iran into the United States of America for a while."
— Former Western military chief

Even a tactically "successful" operation doesn't end with capturing the building. The material itself poses a set of problems with no clean solution — only tradeoffs between contamination, time exposure, transport risk, and whether you've truly eliminated the threat or merely transformed it into a different kind of hazard. Performing this at all three sites would amount to one of the largest raids in military history. The deployment of a Marine Expeditionary Unit to the region suggests contingency planning is underway — but the gap between planning and execution, in this case, is filled with uranium hexafluoride.
